Video & Production Issues

  • Smudged lenses — visible on most published footage
  • Awkward start/end points — every video needs an edit before posting
  • Horizontal (landscape) video on vertical platforms — 100% of the time
  • No intro or outro — viewers have no brand anchor
  • Camera shaking during interviews — phone holder is also the interviewer
  • Animated graphic slideshows replacing real footage — low performance
🔊

Audio Problems

  • Volume swings — too loud in some clips, inaudible in others
  • No consistent microphone used across the team
  • Background gym/ambient noise drowning out voices
  • No trending audio layered under footage
  • Wind noise in outdoor footage — no wind protection
📌

Strategy Gaps

  • Social used as a bulletin board — announcements only, no storytelling
  • Over-reliance on testimonial content without a narrative structure
  • No hook strategy — viewers drop off in the first 2 seconds
  • No CTAs — people engage but don't know what to do next
  • QR codes in posts — remove immediately, they don't work on mobile
  • Content cross-posted without platform customization
  • Facebook heavily favors testimonials without story framing
✅

What Is Working

  • Real, unfiltered energy from staff and kids — this is rare and valuable
  • Wholesome, relatable content moments already being captured
  • Strong programmatic stories exist — they're just buried in unedited footage
  • $1/day price point is compelling — just not being communicated
  • Sports content has built-in shareability and hype potential
  • Increasing momentum visible in recent months of posting

Perception Problems to Fix

  • Perceived as "only for low-income Black kids" — parents confirmed this directly
  • Sports league seen as "bottom shelf" — counter with skill and highlight content
  • Not visible enough to corporate sponsors or two-income households
  • Parents camp overnight for registration spots — this is a viral story, untold
  • Needs to show two-parent households, working professionals, and diverse family types

🎬

The Golden Rule: If the subject is moving — be still. If the subject is still — you can move slowly. This eliminates 80% of shaky, unwatchable footage.

🎙️

Interviews

  • Put phone on tripod — never hold it while interviewing
  • Position subject with light on their face, not behind them
  • Always use the clip-on mic — no exceptions
  • Film at eye level — never look up or down at someone
  • Rule of thirds — subject slightly off-center in the frame
  • Ask them to include the question in their answer: "I love BGC because..." not just "I love it"
  • Record 5–10 extra seconds before and after — give yourself room to edit
🏀

Action & Events

  • Slow pan: move in ONE direction slowly — don't sweep back and forth
  • Hold still on a moment — let something interesting happen in frame
  • Quick hook shot: fast zoom to grab attention in the first 2 seconds
  • Capture close-ups: hands dribbling, pencil writing, food being plated
  • Capture reaction shots — a kid's laugh, a high five, a stunned face
  • Get low angles for sports — makes athletes look powerful
  • Move at walking pace alongside your subject — never run with the camera
✂️

The Edit

  • Every video MUST be edited before posting — no exceptions
  • Start on the most interesting frame — not a slow warmup
  • End cleanly — cut before the video naturally dies
  • Add captions — 85% of viewers watch without sound
  • Text hook on screen in the first 2 seconds
  • BGC logo watermark in a corner — subtle, not distracting
  • Keep it short: 15–45 sec for TikTok/Reels, up to 90 sec for Facebook
🚫

Never Do This

  • Never post horizontal (landscape) video on Instagram or TikTok
  • Never post raw, unedited footage — always trim and add captions
  • Never use QR codes in social posts — they don't work on mobile
  • Never film with a bright light or window behind the subject
  • Never post animated graphic slideshows — use real footage
  • Never post with a smudged or dirty lens — reshoot it
  • Never post without a caption and CTA — tell people what to do next
📝

The Script Rule: You don't need a full script. You need a hook sentence, 3 key points, and an ending CTA. Write it on a sticky note before you film. That's your script.

🪝

You have 1.5 seconds. The hook is the entire game. These 10 hook types should rotate through every video you make — never let your feed feel repetitive.

01
Visual Curiosity Hook

The Slow Reveal

This is a purely visual experience — no talking. Start tight on something unexpected or beautiful: a kid's hands building something, a packed gym floor, a trophy being lifted. Let the camera slowly pull back or cut wider to reveal the full scene. The viewer's brain wants to know what they're looking at — that tension keeps them watching. No words needed for the first 3–5 seconds.
02
Bold Statement Hook

The Myth-Buster

Say out loud — or put on screen — the exact wrong thing people believe about BGC. Then immediately prove them wrong with what you show next. Example: Text on screen says "BGC is just babysitting." Then cut to a kid presenting a coding project. Let the visuals do the arguing. The key is naming the myth confidently, not defensively.
03
Question Hook

The Direct Ask

Ask one simple, specific question that puts the viewer in the story. It must feel personal — not rhetorical. Example: "If you could give your kid a safe place, a hot meal, and a skill — every single day this summer — for $5 a week… would you?" Pause. Let it land. Then show what that looks like at BGC.
04
Viral Challenge Hook

The Skill / Trick Drop

Open on a kid or staff member landing a trick, skill, or unexpected move — basketball behind-the-back pass, speed cup stack, perfect catch on a difficult toss. No buildup. Start at the moment of execution. The shock of the skill IS the hook. End with: "Think you can beat that? Duet us and show us what you've got." This earns Stitches, Duets, and FYP reach.
05
Emotional Hook

The Real Moment

Open on a genuine, unscripted emotional moment — a kid's face when they score, a hug between a staff member and a child, a laugh at the dinner table. Zero words. Zero music for the first 2 seconds. The raw audio of the moment is the hook. This is the hardest hook to fake and the most powerful when it's real.
06
Fast Cut Hook

The Highlight Open

3 of your absolute best clips — cut together in under 3 seconds total. No transition, no fade. Hard cuts only. Fast. Then slow the pace down and tell the story. Think of it like a movie trailer opening — grab them with the best stuff first, then earn their attention for the rest.
07
Relatability Hook

The Parent Pain Point

Start by naming something a working parent worries about — out loud, directly into the camera. Don't soften it. Example: "You get off work at 5. School ends at 3. That's two hours your kid is somewhere — and you don't always know where." Pause. "Here's what BGC does with those two hours." Then show it. The parent who needed to hear this will stop scrolling immediately.
08
Throwback Hook

Same Age, Different Time

A staff member holds up a photo of themselves at the same age as the kids they work with today. No elaborate setup — just hold the photo up to the camera, let it fill the frame for 2 seconds, then pull back so people can see who's holding it. Simple. No words needed. The visual contrast of then vs. now, in the same building, with the same mission — that's the whole story.
09
Stitch Hook

The Floating Head Agree

Find a viral video about expensive youth sports, the childcare crisis, or summer program costs. React to it simply — staff member on screen, pointing or nodding at the clip. No big speech needed. End with one line: "That's why BGC charges $1 a day." Done. The original video's reach carries yours into new audiences.
10
Contrast Hook

Before / After

"This kid could barely read in September." [hold on blank screen or still photo for 1 second] "Here he is now." [cut to him reading aloud with confidence]. The pause between the two halves IS the hook. Give the viewer time to feel the gap before you close it. Works for reading, coding, sports skills, or any visible growth.

Before You Film Anything

How to Find a Story.

Great content doesn't start with a camera — it starts with a question. This section teaches you how to spot a story, shape it, and get ready to film it. Even if you've never done this before.

👁️

The truth about stories: They're already happening at BGC every single day. Your job isn't to create them — it's to recognize them, capture them before they're gone, and frame them so other people care. That's the whole skill.

Step 1 — Spotting a Good Story
👀

The 4 Signals

When you walk into the club, scan the room for these four things. If you see any of them — stop and film.

  • A first: First basket, first time cooking a dish, first day, first win. Firsts are always emotional.
  • A surprise: A kid doing something you didn't expect. A staff member acting out of their comfort zone. Unexpected moments are watchable.
  • A contrast: Something that challenges what people assume about BGC — a young kid explaining code, a shy kid giving a speech, a "tough" kid tearing up over a win.
  • A real relationship: A kid and staff member with visible trust and warmth. That connection on camera stops people mid-scroll.
🚨

Red Flags — This Is NOT a Story

Don't waste your filming time on these — they rarely become good posts.

  • An event happening in the background with nobody reacting to it
  • A kid or staff member who's clearly uncomfortable being filmed
  • A room that's empty or slow — wait for the energy to build
  • Something you'd need a 3-sentence caption to explain — if it can't speak for itself visually, it's not ready yet
  • Anything that feels like a press release — "Today we hosted our annual…" Nobody watches that.
💬

The One-Question Test

Before you pull out your phone, ask yourself one question:

"Would I stop scrolling for this?"

  • If yes — film it immediately, before the moment passes
  • If you're unsure — film a short clip anyway, decide later in editing
  • If no — wait for a better moment or go find one somewhere else in the building
  • Don't overthink it. Filming and deleting costs nothing. Missing the moment costs the story.
Step 2 — Forming the Idea
🧠

Every good piece of content answers one of these three questions: What's happening? → Why does it matter? → What do I want people to feel or do? If you can answer all three in one sentence — you have a post.

Story Idea Builder — Fill This In Before Every Shoot
📋 Use this as a mental checklist or print it — one per shoot. If you can fill in all 6 boxes, you're ready to film.
Step 3 — The Light Storyboard
🎬

A storyboard doesn't have to be complicated. It's just a plan for what you're going to film and in what order. Think of it as a shopping list for your camera. You don't need to draw anything — just write 4–5 bullets before you start filming.

The 5-Shot Framework

Every video needs these five types of shots. If you get all five, you can edit anything.

01
The Hook Shot
The most interesting, surprising, or emotional moment. This goes first in the edit. Film it as close-up as possible.
02
The Wide Shot
Shows where we are and sets the scene. The gym, the classroom, the field. 5–8 seconds.
03
The Detail Shot
Get close. Hands writing, shoes on the court, food on the plate, a face concentrating. These are the shots editors love.
04
The Reaction Shot
A face watching something happen. The crowd. The coach. A parent. The reaction tells viewers how to feel.
05
The Closing Shot
Something that feels like an ending — kids walking away, a handshake, the lights going off, a final laugh. Gives the edit a natural place to land.
Example: Marcus's First 3-Pointer

Here's what a real light storyboard looks like — written on a phone Notes app in 2 minutes before filming:

Hook Shot
Zoom tight on Marcus's face when the ball goes in — get the exact moment of his reaction
Wide Shot
Pan across the gym during warm-ups — show the full team
Detail Shot
Close up on his hands as he practices the shot before the game
Reaction Shot
Coach's reaction + teammates rushing him after the basket
Closing Shot
Marcus walking off the court after the game, smiling — slow motion if possible

Result: 5 shots. 2 minutes of planning. A complete, emotionally compelling post ready to edit — with a natural hook, story, and ending already built in.

➡️

Now you're ready. Once you've spotted a story, formed your idea, and planned your 5 shots — go to the Content Ideas tab to match your story to an existing content type, or the Content Matrix to confirm the hook, persona, and CTA before you post.

Step by Step

The Rollout.

Five phases. Check each task off as your team completes it. Build momentum — don't try to do everything at once.

Phase 1 · Weeks 1–2
Foundation Setup
  • Download CapCut on 2 staff phones — do the 15-minute beginner tutorial together as a team
  • Order clip-on mic: Hollyland Lark M1 or DJI Mic Mini (~$60)
  • Update ALL phone camera settings using the settings tab in the Playbook above
  • Update all social profile bios, profile photos, and "link in bio" — consistent everywhere
  • Create shared Google Drive folder: "BGC Content Library" with subfolders by month
  • Designate one "Content Lead" — responsible for weekly posting and the calendar
  • Print the Capture Rules and post them somewhere visible in the club
  • Do a practice shoot — 3 minutes inside the club. Watch it together and review.
Phase 2 · Weeks 3–4
First Posts Go Live
  • Film and post first Staff Intro Reel — Hook #5 (the real moment)
  • Film the "$1 a Day" video — Andra or Cecilia to camera, clip-on mic required
  • Post first Weekly Basketball Highlight — name the kids on screen
  • Film one parent testimonial — real parent, real story, no script needed
  • Launch the "BGC Is or Is Not" TikTok series — Episode 1
  • Review Week 1 analytics — which post got the most reach? Note it.
Phase 3 · Month 2
Build the System
  • Lock in weekly rhythm: Highlights every Friday, Parent Story every Tuesday
  • Run your first Challenge video — Tower Toss or Cup Stacking Challenge
  • Batch film 3 volunteer stories in one afternoon — more efficient than one at a time
  • Hold first 30-min monthly content meeting — review what worked and what didn't
  • Start using IG Stories for quick behind-the-scenes between bigger posts
  • Start building content bank — capture b-roll daily even when not posting
  • Add a subtle logo outro to all new videos — even just a 2-second hold
Phase 4 · Months 3–4
Summer Sprint
  • Film the overnight registration lineup — this is your highest potential viral moment
  • Document Summer Day 1 thoroughly — capture 20+ clips for the content library
  • Increase to 5 posts per week during summer — the content is right there
  • Run a supervised kid TikTok takeover — with parental permission forms signed
  • Film full field trip day — arrival to departure — as a mini-documentary
  • Introduce more complex edits now: transitions, text animations, music sync
  • Identify 2–3 local family influencer candidates — families willing to share organically
Phase 5 · Months 5–6
Impact & Donor Push
  • Produce a 60–90 second year-end impact video — Andra narrates over b-roll highlights
  • Make specific, named asks: "3 buses. $X each. Here's who to contact."
  • Feature 2 corporate sponsors in spotlight posts — tag them so they'll share it
  • Complete full 6-month analytics review — follows, reach, link clicks, saves
  • Plan Year 2 calendar using what the data showed worked
  • Celebrate your team — 6 months of consistent content is a real achievement. Document it.
🌟

Remember: You don't need to be a social media expert. You need to be consistent, honest, and interest-first. The kids, the staff, and the families are the content. Your job is to point the camera at the right moments — and make sure the lens is clean.
